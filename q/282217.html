This answer uses the following observations: all prime numbers except 2&3 are 1 less or more than a multiple of 6; and primes get sparser as numbers get higher.

We start by setting a cell to 50, then use this to generate every 6th number from 50 down to 2, wrapping round to 252 then down through all multiples of 6 until we reach 6. We then adjust these to make the primes

Most numbers are then tweaked to a prime by adding/subtracting 1. for primes under 50 we have two even numbers available (multiple of 6, and multiple of 6 plus 2) so we always subtract 1, for example 6-1=5, 8-1=7. For numbers above 50 we have only the multiple of 6. Some of these multiples of 6 have 1 prime next to them so we add/subtract 1 as necessary. Some others are between 2 primes so we have a choice of either adding or subtracting 1. We select whichever prime is more difficult to generate in another way. The other prime (the easier one) is generated by adjusting a multiple of 6 which is not next to any prime. In many cases the adjustment is by 5, so there is a loop that runs for 5 iterations to do this.

The first step does not generate sufficient initial values to generate all the primes. Therefore, the first number 50 is used as a loop counter to generate three primes which are close to multiples of 50: 199,149,101. This code also adjusts 26 to 76, which is then tweaked to 73.

Below shows the initial and final states of the tape. Numbers that are adjusted by 1 are marked with a + or -. Other numbers are marked with the size of the adjustment. Numbers that are created in other ways are marked with (). These include 199, 149, 101 which are generated by the 50-iteration loop, as well as 3 which is made by adjusting the depleted counter cell for this loop. The numbers 2 and 251 are generated by the initial loop but 2 is reduced back to zero to act as a pointer stop for the 5-iteration loop and 251 is used as an upcounter, so these two values have to be regenerated once the 5-iteration loop is finished.
